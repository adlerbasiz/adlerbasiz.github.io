Professionalism, MRPBA capabilities and Graduate qualities

Professionalism
Communication capabilities, interprofessional problem-solving and professional ethical behaviour are the three key characteristics of medical radiation professionalism (Bore et al., 2005). Effective connections between radiographers and patients are built on a foundation of advanced communication techniques (Halkett et al., 2010; Pollard et al., 2019). I possess the ability to explain complex imaging procedures in English, Cantonese and Mandarin in an effective and patient-comforting manner. Multilingual radiographers are major assets to radiology departments, as they can overcome the communication barriers that can impact the quality of radiology services and the diagnostic ability of medical images (Choong et al., 2021).
I was explaining the procedure of a contrast-enhanced CT scan to Patient X. Patient X was from a non-English speaking background and refused to sign the consent form. I spotted her consent form was in Chinese, thus I explained the consent required using Mandarin. Patient X expressed her concern, that she had her previous contrast scan one week before. I indicated that she had a satisfactory renal function. Her estimated glomerular filtration rate was far greater than 45, thus the risk of contrast-induced kidney injury is close to nil (Davenport et al., 2020). She was still reluctant to perform the scan but she was less anxious. I negotiated with her that I will halve the contrast media given to her and attempt the scan, and both my supervisor and Patient X agreed to proceed with the scan in this case. The final image quality is acceptable, and Patient X was satisfied that a multilingual radiographer with good communication skills was present in this practice and the solution was figured out eventually. In this case, consent was obtained with the consideration of patient autonomy and safety, which is an ethical practice (Haskell, 2019).
As a student radiographer, I understand my limitation that I need further experience to be more confident in my practice. I was assigned to perform x-rays in a urology case alone and I arrived at the theatre's front door with the fluoroscopy machine. The circulating nurse spotted my arrival and advised me to wait until the patient was draped, to better protect patient privacy. The patient was uncovered on the table for surgical site disinfection, opening the front door to push the machine in during that time might cause a breach of patient confidentiality. Theatre staff have the duty to identify circumstances or actions that could violate patient privacy (Blomberg et al., 2015). Therefore, I waited until the patient was covered and then brought the machine in for intraoperative imaging. In this case, I learnt actively and accepted advice from other experienced medical professionals to improve my professionalism in medical imaging.
Interprofessional learning has benefited my experience in ethical practices, moreover, effective interprofessional communication and active listening were involved to deliver ethical and patient-oriented care. Examples of my interprofessional learning include interventional CT imaging with radiologists, swallow test imaging with speech pathologists, intraoperative imaging, mobile imaging, a multi-disciplinary care plan development event named Health Collaboration Challenge etc. In order to be a competent radiographer in the future, I will continue to work on my communication and ethical skills.
MRPBA capabilities
Medical imaging practice must be constantly backed by evidence (Hoffmann et al., 2017). Medical radiation practitioners are obliged to recognize any problem or uncertainty they have in their practice and seek an answer via research (Medical Radiation Practice Board of Australia, 2020). I was undertaking a project about aseptic techniques in handling pressure injectors for the delivery of contrast agents. Pressure injectors are present in most computed tomography [CT] environments (Ma et al., 2012), thus contrast-enhanced CT scans can be carried out. In my previous placement sites, I observed that the healthcare workers do not have a uniform protocol for teaching students about injector machines and administering contrast. Student practitioners could access the pressure injectors without an introductory session offered by the supervisors. In addition, when I was seeking advice related to pressure injectors, different supervisors had different measures for maintaining the sterility of the content inside the injectors. I was doing research online after working hours so that I could find out the correct method to approach the injector machines, but the materials I found might not be reliable in best guaranteeing patient safety.
This brought my attention to the underlying issues in the aspects of infection prevention and control. In my literature review, blood-borne diseases are occasionally transmitted in clinical environments due to inappropriate injection techniques (Khan et al., 2017). In studies investigating the origin of several clusters of hospital-acquired infections (Balmelli et al., 2020; Patel et al., 2006), reusing the same disposable item on the same or a different patient happened frequently, including but not limited to the procedures of administering contrast. Moreover, an incorrect dirty-to-clean approach could have been used, staff might detach the injector disposable extension line from the patient first, and then detach the line from the machine without changing gloves in between the steps (Pa√±ella et al., 2008). These maneuvers might be taught to and performed by student practitioners and result in a breach of infection prevention and control [IPC] standards. Ethical medical radiation practitioners should prioritize patient safety under all circumstances; therefore, it is essential to conduct research on this topic and support IPC education and practice with more evidence. I will conduct a survey to find out the level of student involvement in assisting with the set-up of the contrast delivery system, also whether their routine set-up and management follow the IPC standard. My study serves as a component of another core project related to infection control in radiography.
Within this project, I acquired basic knowledge of the main steps in the research pathway, from study method to data analysis and publication. The project sharpens my critical thinking skills and path my future by fulfilling the need for professional development. As a novice researcher, I still have a large gap for improvement. The assistance from supervising professors and lecturers is of utmost importance, they provide constructive feedback on my research studies and the advice benefited my research skills as well my inventiveness.
Graduate qualities
Inventiveness and influence are listed in the nine qualities that top universities expect students to graduate with (The University of Sydney, 2020). Inventiveness is the process of developing innovative concepts and approaches in radiography, halving the contrast given to Patient X as aforementioned can demonstrate my ability to solve unexpected challenges via creative ideas and methods that are practical. For example, I was performing intraoperative imaging without supervisors and my fluoroscopy machine experienced a breakdown. I attempted to reboot the system but in vain. In this situation, I was expected to make a call down to the radiology department for assistance, but in order not to disturb the surgery progress I decided to mobilize other non-sterile medical personnel and bring in the spare fluoroscopy machine. The surgery was completed without delay and the surgeon was satisfied that the issue was managed well. In another example, I was performing modified foot x-rays on a wheelchair patient with extensive swelling around his foot. As he could not tolerate any hard surface including the image receptor [IR] board, I performed the x-rays with a tiny object image distance. I placed the image receptor on the floor, and he was in his wheelchair with his foot hanging slightly above the IR.
IR position and tube angle were adjusted accordingly. The lateral foot x-ray was taken with an IR holder on the medial side of the foot. Image quality was acceptable and patient discomfort was minimized. Influence is the connection and engagement with others in different ideas and visions (The University of Sydney, 2020). As a student radiographer, I am constantly improving and influenced by lecturers and supervisors positively. I was explaining to junior radiography students in my clinical site, particularly in the operating theatres and CT scanner rooms. As junior students are less experienced in advanced modalities, I was assigned to give a general introduction of theatre and CT environment to them. In addition, I showed them my basic maneuvers and gave them attempts to perform some simple tasks, including driving the fluoroscopy machine from the equipment room to the theatre suite, raising or lowering the CT bed etc. These simple tasks were effective for students to work around the role of the radiographers in theatre and CT environment, and to arouse their interest in getting involved with advanced modalities. Regarding my research project, I anticipate that my publication can influence other medical radiation practitioners positively by providing evidence-based advice focusing on the IPC in contrast delivery system.
Additionally, it can raise IPC awareness among other healthcare workers and specifically the staff in the radiology department. The application of the IPC knowledge in my future placement sites and workplaces will influence the other student radiographers and colleagues As a student radiographer, I have to obtain more clinical experience to further advance my inventiveness in other modalities. I also need more participation in the university mentorship of junior student practitioners and create a stronger positive impact on the medical radiation society and the medical field.
